Snake Body Lab

Special Snake Features

Snakes have been slithering around Earth for over 100 million years—long before humans ever showed up! These amazing reptiles come in all shapes and sizes, from tiny threadsnakes that can fit on a pencil to giant anacondas as long as a school bus. They live almost everywhere in the world, from scorching deserts to deep oceans.

One of the coolest things about snakes is their unique body structure. Unlike us, snakes don't have legs or arms. Instead, they have a long, flexible body covered in scales that help them move smoothly through their environment. Their backbone is made up of many small bones called vertebrae, which give them incredible flexibility and strength.

Snakes also have some fascinating adaptations that make them expert hunters. Here are some key features of snake anatomy:

Scales
Snakes are covered in scales that protect their skin and help them move. The scales can be smooth or keeled (ridged), and they come in a variety of colors and patterns that help with camouflage.
Jacobson's Organ
Snakes use their forked tongues to "smell" the air. They flick their tongues in and out to collect scent particles, which they then analyze using a special organ called the Jacobson's organ located in the roof of their mouth.
Heat-Sensing Pits
Some snakes, like pit vipers, have special heat-sensing pits on their faces that allow them to detect warm-blooded prey even in complete darkness.
Venom Glands
Venomous snakes have glands that produce venom, which they use to immobilize or kill their prey. The venom is delivered through specialized fangs when the snake bites.
Flexible Jaw
Snakes have incredibly flexible jaws that allow them to swallow prey much larger than their head. Their lower jaw is not fused at the chin, enabling them to stretch it wide open.
Muscular Body
Snakes rely on strong muscles to move. They use different types of locomotion, such as lateral undulation (side-to-side movement), rectilinear (straight-line movement), and concertina (accordion-like movement) to navigate various terrains.
Glottis
The glottis is a small opening in the snake's throat that allows it to breathe while swallowing large prey. It can extend outwards, ensuring the snake can continue to get air even when its mouth is full.
Spectacle
Snakes don’t have eyelids. Instead, their eyes are covered by a clear scale called a spectacle, which protects the eye and keeps it from drying out.
Cloaca
The cloaca is a single opening near the base of the tail used for waste, laying eggs or giving birth, and reproduction.

Big Belly: Body Expansion

Once their prey is inside, a snake’s body needs to make room. Their stretchy skin and flexible vertebrae (the bones in their backbone) allow their body to expand, turning them into a living “lump conveyor belt” as the meal slowly moves down toward the stomach.

How Snakes Move

Even though they don’t have legs, snakes are amazing movers! They can twist, turn, climb, swim, and slither in ways that would be impossible for most animals. Snakes use their strong, flexible muscles and hundreds of tiny belly scales to grip the ground, push themselves forward, or even climb straight up trees.

They can travel in several different ways:

  • Straight-line crawling: Some snakes use their belly muscles to move forward in a smooth, straight line, almost like a conveyor belt.
  • Bunching and stretching: Other snakes bunch up their body, then stretch it out again to pull themselves forward—kind of like an accordion.
  • Looping and sidewinding: On slippery sand or loose dirt, some snakes throw parts of their body into loops and “toss” themselves sideways in a special move called sidewinding.
  • Swimming: Freshwater and sea snakes use powerful side-to-side movements to glide through the water. Sea snakes even have paddle-shaped tails that act like boat oars to help them swim faster.

Cold-Blooded and Cozy

Snakes are ectothermic, or cold-blooded, which means they can’t make their own body heat like mammals and birds. Instead, they rely on their environment to stay warm. When it’s chilly, snakes will bask in the sun to soak up heat. When it’s too hot, they’ll seek shade or burrow underground to cool off. This is why you often see snakes out on sunny rocks or roads in the early morning or late afternoon.

Scaly Suits and Shedding

Snakes are covered in tough, protective scales that help them move and keep their skin from drying out. As snakes grow, they need to shed their old skin to make way for a new, larger one. This process is called molting or shedding. Before shedding, a snake’s skin may look dull or cloudy, and its eyes might appear bluish. When it’s time to shed, the snake will rub against rough surfaces to help peel off the old skin, revealing a shiny new layer underneath.
