Snakes in Mythology

Mythology
A collection of old stories that people used to explain the world around them.

Snakes as Symbols of Healing and Life

In many places, snakes were seen as creatures connected to healing and new beginnings. This is because snakes shed their old skin and grow a shiny new one, almost like they’re being “reborn.”

  • In Hindu mythology, snakes are linked to eternity and the continuous cycle of life, because the giant serpent Shesha holds up the universe with its endless coils.
  • In African cultures, snakes are linked to fertility (the ability to make new life) and farming, because people noticed snakes coming out after rain.
  • In Native American stories, snakes represent change and transformation because of their skin shedding.

Snakes as Protectors and Powerful Beings

Sometimes snakes were thought of as guardians or symbols of royalty and wisdom.

  • In ancient Egypt, the cobra was seen as a protector. Pharaohs (kings and queens) wore crowns decorated with cobras to show their power.
  • In Chinese culture, the snake is one of the twelve zodiac animals and is admired for being wise, mysterious, and clever.
  • In Mesoamerica (Aztecs and Mayans), a god called Quetzalcoatl was pictured as a giant feathered serpent. He was believed to bring knowledge and creation.

Snakes as Creators of the World

In some stories, snakes are seen as giant beings that helped shape the Earth.

  • In Hindu mythology, the massive serpent Shesha holds up the entire universe on its heads, showing balance and eternity (forever).
  • In Australian Aboriginal traditions, the Rainbow Serpent was a creator spirit that shaped rivers and land, bringing water and life.
  • In some African tales, a giant snake is said to have created the world by coiling around it and forming mountains and valleys.

Snakes as Symbols of Chaos and Danger

Not all stories about snakes are positive. In many cultures, snakes are also seen as dangerous or even destructive creatures.

  • In Norse mythology, Jörmungandr, the Midgard Serpent, was so huge it wrapped around the world. When it let go, people believed the world would end in chaos.
  • In the Bible’s story of Adam and Eve, a snake tempts Eve to eat fruit from the forbidden tree, leading to trouble for humans.
  • In Greek mythology, the monster Medusa had snakes for hair. Anyone who looked at her was instantly turned to stone!

Snakes in Art

Art
How people express ideas and feelings through drawings, paintings, sculpture, and more.

Snakes in Ancient Art

Snakes have been featured in art for thousands of years, often symbolizing important ideas.

  • In ancient Egypt, snakes were often shown in jewelry and carvings, symbolizing protection and royalty.
  • In ancient Greece, snakes appeared in pottery and sculptures, often linked to healing and medicine.
  • In Mesoamerican art, snakes were depicted in murals and sculptures, representing gods and creation stories.

Snakes in Modern Art

Even today, artists use snakes in their work to explore themes of transformation, danger, and beauty.

  • Contemporary artists often use snake imagery to discuss themes of change and rebirth, inspired by the snake’s ability to shed its skin.
  • Snakes are also used in fashion and design, symbolizing elegance and mystery.
  • In tattoos, snake designs are popular for their symbolism of strength, protection, and transformation.

Snakes in Culture

Culture
the way of life for a group of people, including their traditions, stories, and beliefs.

Snakes in Festivals and Traditions

Many cultures celebrate snakes through festivals and traditions that highlight their importance.

  • In India, the festival of Nag Panchami honors snakes with offerings and prayers for protection and blessings.
  • In Mexico, the Day of the Dead sometimes features snake imagery, symbolizing the cycle of life and death.
  • In some African communities, snake dances are performed to celebrate fertility and the coming of rain.

Snakes in Folklore and Superstitions

Snakes often appear in folk tales and superstitions, reflecting how people view them in everyday life.

  • In many cultures, seeing a snake is considered a sign of good luck or a message from the spiritual world.
  • Some superstitions warn against harming snakes, believing it could bring bad luck or misfortune.
  • In various folk tales, snakes are portrayed as wise creatures that can offer guidance or protection to those who respect them.

Snakes in Medicine

Medicine
the science of healing and keeping people healthy.

Snake Venom in Medicine

Snake venom, while dangerous, has been studied for its potential to help treat various medical conditions.

  • Some components of snake venom are used to develop medicines for heart disease, blood clots, and pain relief.
  • Research is ongoing to explore how snake venom can be used in cancer treatment and other serious illnesses.
  • Antivenoms, which are made from snake venom, are crucial for treating snake bites and saving lives.

Snakes in Medical Symbols

Snakes have long been associated with medicine and healing, often appearing in medical symbols.

  • The Rod of Asclepius, a staff with a single snake wrapped around it, is a widely recognized symbol of medicine and healthcare.
  • The Caduceus, featuring two snakes entwined around a winged staff, is often mistakenly used as a medical symbol but is actually associated with commerce and negotiation.
  • These symbols highlight the historical connection between snakes and the field of medicine, emphasizing themes of healing and protection.

Snakes in Literature

Literature
Written works like stories, poems, and books.

Snakes in Classic Literature

Snakes have been featured in many classic stories and books, often symbolizing important themes.

  • In the Bible, the snake in the Garden of Eden represents temptation and the fall of humanity.
  • In "The Jungle Book" by Rudyard Kipling, the snake Kaa is a wise and powerful character who helps the protagonist, Mowgli.
  • In "The Hobbit" by J.R.R. Tolkien, the dragon Smaug, who is similar to a giant snake, represents greed and destruction.

Snakes in Modern Literature

In contemporary books and stories, snakes continue to be popular symbols for various themes.

  • In the "Harry Potter" series by J.K. Rowling, the snake is associated with the dark wizard Voldemort and his followers, symbolizing evil and danger.
  • In "Life of Pi" by Yann Martel, a snake represents both fear and survival as the protagonist faces challenges on a lifeboat.
  • Snakes often appear in fantasy and adventure stories, symbolizing mystery, transformation, and the unknown.
